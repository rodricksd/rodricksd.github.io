Being SheEO — No. 2

Before You Can Lead a Team, You Have to Choose One.

Deandra Rodricks June 6, 2026 4 min read
Narration 4:35

Nobody tells you that the first real test of leadership is not a strategy deck or a boardroom decision. It is a spreadsheet with names on it and the weight of knowing that where you place people will shape everything that follows next. That was my week one!

I had just been appointed CEO of this simulated startup. The team I was building was not made up of strangers I found on LinkedIn. These were my cohort: people I had studied with, struggled through group projects with, and sat across from in class for months. I knew how they thought. I knew their strengths. And I knew exactly where my biases were, which meant I had to work twice as hard to keep them out of the process.

Before making any decisions, I first took a step back. I mapped everyone against their strongest skills, deliberately and methodically, and built the org structure from there: C-suite, middle management, individual contributors. I am not filling seats. I am trying to build something that will actually function.

One of the things I had learned here in a Purdue class and genuinely believe is that power shifts. Leading with authority might get short-term results, but it does not build a team. So, when someone pushed back on their role, I listened. I took it seriously. But I did not move them just because it was uncomfortable. In one case, someone wanted to be placed on a team that felt more familiar to them, more within what they already knew. I kept my decision. Not to prove a point, but because I believed being stretched was exactly what they needed. Growth rarely happens in the places that feel safe. I was not exempt from that either.

There were other adjustments too. People left. New people joined. Just like you would expect in a real startup. I made the best decisions I could with the information I had, and I moved forward. What I did not do was unravel every time something shifted.

Once the team was set, I had to figure out what my role actually looked like day to day. And this was the part that caught me off guard.

Every leadership role I had held before, whether in a student organization, a group project, or a work setting, meant being actively in the work. Contributing, creating, producing. My fingerprints were directly on the output. This is different. My job is not to build the product. My job is to hold the vision clearly enough that my team can build it.

I gave them everything I had: the product direction, the goals, the reasoning behind decisions. I set up meeting cadences, help define how each function connects to the others, and then I step back. I let them make the calls. I let them own it, as I believe they should!

What I keep for myself is the accountability layer. Making sure things are moving, checking in where they are not, having the harder conversations when someone is not showing up the way the team needs. And that last part, the wrangling, turns out to be its own skill set entirely.

What I have learned and know now is this: when you lead with transparency and set clear expectations from the start, friction decreases. Not disappears, decreases. The approach of I am the CEO, do as I say might get compliance. It will not get ownership. And ownership is what builds something worth building.

Trusting my team has made me more confident in my own decisions. When people feel like the project is theirs, because it is, they show up differently. That energy is real and it is contagious. I am building shortlist, a job search management platform, alongside an incredibly talented team of Purdue MBT students.

Every decision I make, every conversation I navigate, every time I hold my ground when it would be easier not to, I am becoming the leader I always envisioned myself to be. This part is not a simulation. This is the work. Women have always built things. Now I am one of them by being SheEO.

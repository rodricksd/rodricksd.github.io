Five Mistakes Organizations Make When Building an AI Governance Program

May 28, 2026 6 min read GRC & AI Strategy

Most organizations building AI governance programs for the first time are doing it under pressure — a regulatory deadline, a board-level mandate, or a near-miss incident that made the risk suddenly legible to people who weren't paying attention. That pressure creates predictable failure modes. These are the five I see most consistently, and what to do instead.

1. Starting with policy instead of inventory

The instinct is understandable: governance means documentation, documentation means policy, so build the policy first. The problem is that an AI policy written before you know what AI systems your organization is actually running is a policy built on assumptions. And in AI governance, assumptions are where liability lives.

The first deliverable of any credible AI governance program is an AI inventory — a registry of every AI system in use, whether built internally, procured from a vendor, or embedded in an existing platform. You cannot manage what you haven't mapped. Start there.

The hardest part of building the AI inventory isn't the systems IT knows about. It's the tools individual teams adopted on their own, the vendor platforms with AI features quietly enabled, and the models your data science team built and pushed to production without a formal review process.

2. Treating AI risk as IT risk

This mistake usually looks like assigning AI governance ownership entirely to the security or IT function, placing AI in the existing IT risk register without modification, and calling it covered. It isn't — for reasons covered in detail in the previous articles in this series.

The distinct risks AI introduces — algorithmic bias, model drift, explainability gaps, unintended emergent behavior — require governance ownership that spans legal, compliance, data science, and the business units deploying the technology. IT is an essential stakeholder, not the sole owner. Building a governance structure that reflects this cross-functional reality is non-negotiable for programs that will actually hold up.

3. Buying a framework and calling it a program

Frameworks — NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001, the EU AI Act compliance requirements — are starting points. They tell you what categories of risk to think about and what organizational capabilities to build. They don't tell you what your specific AI systems actually do, what risks they carry in your specific operating context, or what controls are proportionate to your risk level.

Organizations that point to a framework adoption as evidence of governance maturity, without doing the work of actually implementing the controls the framework requires, have a false sense of security. Framework alignment is table stakes. The actual governance work is what happens after you adopt the framework.

4. Governing AI point-in-time instead of continuously

A common pattern: an AI system gets reviewed during deployment, passes its initial assessment, and then operates for months or years without meaningful governance oversight. The problem is that AI systems change over time in ways that static assessments don't capture. Models drift as the data they're scoring against shifts. Performance degrades on subpopulations. The business context that made a deployment decision appropriate evolves.

AI governance requires ongoing monitoring — not just of security posture, but of model performance, output distributions, and business alignment. Building this monitoring cadence, and establishing clear thresholds for when a system needs to be reassessed or retired, is one of the most operationally mature things an AI governance program can do. It's also one of the most commonly skipped.

5. Making governance invisible to the people making AI decisions

AI governance programs that live entirely inside compliance and legal functions, communicated down to business units as a set of requirements to satisfy before deployment, will consistently lose the race against the speed of adoption. By the time governance review is complete, the tool is already in use.

The most effective programs I've seen invest heavily in making governance legible to non-technical stakeholders and operationally lightweight for the teams most likely to be deploying AI. That means self-service risk tiering tools, clear intake processes, and a governance function that positions itself as an accelerant for responsible adoption rather than a barrier to any adoption. If the people making AI decisions view governance as overhead to route around, you haven't built a governance program — you've built a speed bump.

Key Takeaways

  • Build the AI inventory before building the policy — you can't govern what you haven't mapped
  • AI risk is not IT risk; governance ownership must be cross-functional, not siloed in security or IT
  • Framework adoption is not program implementation — the real governance work happens after the framework is chosen
  • AI governance must be continuous; point-in-time assessments miss model drift and evolving business context
  • Governance programs that business teams route around have already failed — legibility and operational ease are design requirements, not nice-to-haves

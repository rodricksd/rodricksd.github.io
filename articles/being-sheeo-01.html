Being SheEO — No. 1

I didn't plan to be a CEO. I just didn't say no.

Deandra Rodricks May 30, 2026 4 min read

After undergrad, I fell into the rhythm that a lot of us do: the 8-to-5, head down, show up and deliver. And honestly? I didn't hate it. I loved learning, I loved growing in my role, and being a woman in tech who had secured a solid technical position felt grounding. It felt like proof. But somewhere along the way, I stopped pushing. I got comfortable, and comfortable quietly became complacent.

What saved me was curiosity. My role at the time gave me access I didn't fully appreciate at first: I was working alongside leadership, sitting across from customers, watching how decisions actually got made inside a business. That exposure lit something up. I wanted to understand both sides: the technology and the business building it. I couldn't choose between the two, and then I didn't have to. When Purdue's MBT program came across my radar, something just clicked. I knew it was right before I could even explain why. So, I packed up the 8-to-5 and headed to grad school.

What happened next, I didn't expect: I found myself again. At Purdue I stopped playing small. I led projects, founded and led an organization within my own program, presented with the kind of confidence I had quietly talked myself out of for years. I built real friendships, worked with professors, leaned into every resource the school offered: Business Career Services, mentors, all of it. I was busy in the best possible way. I wasn't just checking boxes, I was actually building something: a version of myself I recognized.

What I didn't know was that people were watching.

I was close to the finish line, starting to think about what I wanted my final project to look like before graduation: something that pulled together everything I'd learned, something that actually meant something. That's when I got an email from the managing director of the MBT program. He wanted to chat about a potential summer project.

We met in person. He walked me through the idea: a cohort-run, company-style project, built and operated like a real startup. I listened and my first instinct, honestly, was that it sounded too technical for me. I said something along those lines. He paused and said:

"Maybe, but I actually want you to run it. I want you to be the CEO."

I think I had the biggest smile. Nerves and excitement hit at the same time, and underneath both of those was something quieter: personal pride. He had seen something in me, and he chose me.

Before I answered, I had a flash, quick and almost involuntary, of every time I had said no to something because I didn't think I was ready, or good enough, or the right fit. Every opportunity I'd talked myself out of before anyone else got the chance to.

This time I didn't do that. I said yes.

The title CEO is one I love. But SheEO? That one actually fits. It's been two weeks and the learning curve is real: I'm not here to solve every problem, I'm here to lead a team well enough that they feel empowered to solve them. It's a different kind of hard. And it's exhilarating in its own way.

This is article one of a series I'm calling Being SheEO, where I'll be writing honestly about what it looks like to go from grad student to CEO, and what it means to be a woman building something in tech.
